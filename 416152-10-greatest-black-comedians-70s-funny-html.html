10 greatest black comedians from the 70s who were so funny

Posted by Martina Birk on Saturday, August 31, 2024

What would the world be without black comedy? Black comedians from the 70s have brought laughter and joy to audiences all over the world. You will be surprised to know that some of these comedians have continued to feature on the top comedian lists even as newer generation comedians emerge.

Black comedy has a long history both in the US and abroad. The black American entertainers had to fight hard in the twentieth century to break the barriers to entry in the comic industry. However, as the world continued to mature both politically and culturally in the 1960s, there emerged an explosion of black comedians worldwide, especially in the 1970s.

List of the greatest black comedians from the 70s

They say that laughter is the best medicine. Whether strictly stand up black comedians or black comedy actors who solely star in films of the comedy genre, African Americans have come a long way to almost dominating the comic world. Here is a look at some of the all-time famous black comedians from the 70s.

1. Bill Cosby

Top on the list of black male stand-up comedians is Bill Cosby. Bill started his career as a stand-up comedian in San Francisco in the 1960s. His debut on NBC's The Tonight Show in 1963 led to a recording contract with Warner Brothers and the release of a series of award-winning comedy albums.

In 1965, he had become the first black actor to star in a drama series after he was cast to play a role in the espionage show I Spy. The Cosby Show, which was launched by NBC in 1984 and aired for eight seasons, was his biggest success and established him as a household name. After that, Cosby embarked on a number of projects, including film roles.

Where is Bill Cosby Now? He is currently serving his jail term at the state correctional institute in Phoenix, Pennsylvania, after being found guilty of three counts of aggravated indecent assault. He was sentenced to a total of three to 10 years in state prison.

2. Rudy Ray Moore

Rudy Ray Moore is a living legend and a cultural treasure to the black community. He is one of the first black comedians. At first, he worked as an entertainer but not a comedian. He was also an R&B singer and released a few songs in the 1940s before he decided to take up comedy.

The biggest break in his career came in 1970, following the release of the comedy LP Eat Out More Often featuring the "Dolemite" toast. He released more comedy albums within a few years, though none achieved the massive acclaim of his first two groundbreaking albums. Moore's comedy albums were always recorded at his home instead of in a club.

Moore refused to let health issues get in his way and continued to entertain audiences until he passed on due to complications of diabetes on October 19, 2008. His influence on popular culture still resonates today and will continue to be felt for generations to come.

3. George Wallace

George Wallace is one of the greatest black comedians. Since his early teenage years, George dreamed of becoming a comedian. Wallace started acting when one of his clients opened a comedy club. His natural humour amused the club owner, and he offered him a chance to perform stand-up comedy.

In 1978, Wallace moved to the West Coast, where he quickly became recognized as a talented young comedian. After one of his performances, producers from the Redd Foxx Show asked him to write for the popular series. He became a regular at the famous Comedy Store, which also featured comedians such as Richard Pryor, Rodney Dangerfield.

Wallace was named the Best Male Standup Comedian during the 1995 American Comedy Awards. He also won the Best Stand Up Comedian award at the prestigious American Comedy Awards after four consecutive nominations in 2013.

4. Paul Mooney

The name Paul Mooney consistently features on the list of black stand-up comedians. He started performing his stand-up comedy while still in high school. Paul met his best friend and stand-up comic Richard Pryor in the 60s, and by the early 70s, Paul was already making appearances in movies, TV shows.

Mooney served as the head writer on The Richard Pryor Show and co-wrote some of Pryor's material on several of his comedy albums and his Saturday Night Live sketches. Mooney also wrote for Sanford and Son, Good Times, In Living Color, The Larry Sanders Show, Chappelle's Show, and Real Husbands of Hollywood.

He collaborated with comedians such as Redd Foxx, Eddie Murphy and many others. In addition to his stand-up comedy, he also featured in comedy movies such as The Buddy Holly Story and Hollywood Shuffle. Sadly, Mooney is no more as he passed on in May 2021 after suffering a heart attack.

5. Richard Pryor

Richard Franklin Lennox Thomas Pryor, famously known as Richard Pryor, is one of the old-school black comedians. In the midst of growing racial tension in the 1960s, comedian Richard Pryor began limiting his self-expression to comedy that didn't touch on religion, politics or race relations.

He started out in the 1960s and was all set for mainstream success until he realized one night on stage in Las Vegas that he was not being true to himself. Richard walked off the stage without performing. He made a comeback two years later, and when he returned, he became not just a genius comic but a major star starring in films like Stir Crazy and Greased Lightning.

Richard died on December 12, 2005, from a heart attack after he suffered multiple sclerosis. He is considered to be one of the most influential stand-up comedians of all time. Richard Pryor's impact on the craft of comedy and today's top comics is legendary and unrivalled.

6. Redd Foxx

The list of funny black men in the 1970s can never be complete without mentioning John Elroy Sanford, famous as Redd Foxx. Foxx was one of the most iconic faces behind the black cultural movement of the 60s and 70s. There was always a big laugh when Foxx told a joke. He became famous during the 1950s and 60s with his nightclub comedy performances.

By joking about everything from sex to colour barriers, he brought simmering and taboo issues into the open. While he was performing in Los Angeles, he was offered a deal with the Dooto record label. In the years that followed, he produced over 50 comedic albums. In 1970 he gave a memorable comic performance in the hit film Cotton Comes to Harlem.

Foxx also launched his own show, The Redd Foxx Comedy Hour, in 1977. He also made appearances in Sanford and Son, Harlem Nights, and The Royal Family. Sadly, while on the set of The Royal Family, Foxx suffered a massive heart attack and died on October 11, 1991.

7. Flip Wilson

Clerow Wilson, famously know as Flip Wilson, was the first black comedian to star in a successful variety TV show. He started working his way up the comedy ranks in the 1950s, but his big break came in 1965 when he was identified as the funniest comedian on The Tonight Show. In 1970, he started his own variety and sketch-comedy show, The Flip Wilson Show.

This show incorporated sketches along with music and celebrity guests. The trailblazing series was a groundbreaking success as it was one of the most-watched shows in America. He also appeared in Saturday Night Live, People Are Funny, Charlie and Co., and The Drew Carey Show. Sadly, he succumbed to cancer in 1998 at the age of 64 years.

8. Dick Gregory

Dick Gregory broke ground in the 1960s as the first mainstream black stand-up comedian. He began his career as a late-night emcee at the black-owned Herman Roberts Show Bar in Chicago in 1958. Dick made his big break at the Playboy Club in 1961 as a one-night replacement and rocketed his way to fame using his unique brand of razor-sharp social satire.

He also made his first appearance on The Tonight Show in 1962, and he became the first black performer invited to sit and talk with the host on air after his performance. Regarded as the first African-American comic to perform regularly in front of white audiences, Gregory appeared on all of the top TV talk shows of the 1960s and 1970s.

His fame drove him to become a public supporter of civil rights and an opponent of the Vietnam War. He was also among the first blacks with their names on the ballot to run for the presidency in the United States in 1968. In his later years, he focused more on health and fitness matters. Unfortunately, Gregory is no more as he passed on in 2017.

9. John Witherspoon

John Witherspoon is best known for his role as the crotchety dad in the Friday films. However, his first roles were on 1970s TV shows such as Barnaby Jones. Later, he appeared in Good Times, What's Happening, and The Incredible Hulk, among others. He also became a regular on The Richard Pryor Show.

His feature films include Hollywood Shuffle, Vampire in Brooklyn, and many others. John is no more as he passed on in 2019, aged 77 years. 10. Garrett Morris

Garrett Isaac Morris, famously known as Garrett Morris, is one of the old school stand-up comedians of the 1970s. He was part of the original cast of the sketch comedy program Saturday Night Live, appearing from 1975 to 1980.

Morris also had a long-running role as Uncle Junior on the sitcom The Jamie Foxx Show. He had starring roles as Earl Washington on the CBS sitcom 2 Broke Girls and Stan Winters in the sitcom Martin. His signature smile has thrilled audiences for more than four decades.
