8 Real-Life Houses Featured in Famous Paintings

Posted by Aldo Pusey on Thursday, March 21, 2024

1. Christina's World, Andrew Wyeth

AlamyLeland Jackson via Flickr

Andrew Wyeth was a close friend of Christina and Alvaro Olsen, who owned the Cushing, Maine home that would be immortalized in Wyeth's famous 1948 painting Christina's World. It was also depicted in numerous lesser-known paintings by the artist, who was allowed to wander through the house at leisure and even operated a studio out of an upstairs room. The Olsen House is now owned and operated by the Farnsworth Art Museum.

2. American Gothic, Grant Wood

Print Collector//Getty Images

Joanna Poe via Flickr

A surprisingly modest Carpenter Gothic home in Eldon, Iowa inspired one of America's most famous 20th-century paintings (only the house appeared in preliminary sketches of the painting). Today, it functions as the American Gothic House Center, offering props to road trippers looking to fluff their Instagram feeds with their own interpretations of the famous pose.

3. A Home on the Mississippi, Currier and Ives

Currier and Ives [Public domain], via Wikimedia CommonsMark Boster//Getty Images

Fans of Southern Comfort are more than a little familiar with the Currier & Ives Lithograph A Home on the Mississippiâ€”it graced the bottle of the popular liquor from the end of the Prohibition Era until the company was rebranded in 2010. Though Currier and Ives are most often associated with the piece, their lithograph was based on an original 1871 work by Alfred Waud. The famous mansion shown in the scene is the privately-owned Woodland Plantation in West Point a La Hache, Louisiana, and it appears today much as it did in Waud's time.

4. Lighthouse at Two Lights, Edward Hopper

AlamyCliff via Flickr

Edward Hopper could often be elusive when it came to subject matter (the setting of his most famous painting, Nighthawks at the Diner, is most certainly a composite of multiple New York City buildings). But in the case of his 1929 work Lighthouse at Two Lights, Hopper depicted Cape Elizabeth's Two Lights Lighthouse much as it actually existsâ€”as a shining, solitary beacon, and to Hopper (who summered in Maine), a symbol of a calm life far away from the chaos of the big city.

6. Stockbridge Main Street at Christmas, Norman Rockwell

assachusetts Office of Travel & Tourism Follow via Flickr

Main Street in Stockbridge, Massachusetts looks nearly identical to the way it was portrayed in Norman Rockwell's aptly-titled 1967 work. But that's no surpriseâ€”the artist knew it well, having lived in Stockbridge for the last 25 years of his life. Make sure to visit the town at Christmas, when it recreates the painting to a tee, right down to the vintage automobiles parked exactly where they belong.

7. The Old Checkered House, Grandma Moses

Richard S. Clayton/<a href="http://www.CambridgePhoto.com" target="_blank">CambridgePhoto.com

If not for Grandma Moses, the local landmark known as the Checkered House (which burned down in 1907) might be a forgotten slice of Cambridge, New York's history. Thankfully, we have many ways to remember the charming one-time inn, which the renowned American folk artist painted numerous times in various seasons. The Checkered House series is perhaps Moses' most well-known collections of paintings.

8. Bedroom at Arles, Vincent Van Gogh

Heritage Images//Getty ImagesTony Hisgett via Flickr

Arguably the most recognizable bedroom in the world was occupied by Vincent Van Gogh during his years in Arles, France (the room was recently replicated as an Airbnb). The famous space was inside a small yellow house on the Place Lamartine in which Van Gogh lived beginning in 1888, and which served as his beloved "Studio of the South." The house was damaged during World War II and later demolished, but it lives on in at least two well-known works by the artist: The Bedroom at Arles and The Yellow House (aka The Street). Today, a four-story building stands in its place.
